Age Seven — Part 3

The next day I went to the chief’s mansion with Giselle.

I wasn’t exactly comfortable asking the chief to be my teacher. I didn’t know him well, and to be honest he made me a little uneasy.

Still — as a magic teacher in this settlement, there was probably no one more suitable than the chief. His instruction would no doubt be strict, but bring it on.

Just as we approached the wooden fence to enter, a voice called from behind us.

“Oh? Isn’t that Abel, the Otem maniac? What are you doing coming to my house?”

I turned to the speaker and found a girl my age standing there. Her sharp red eyes looked like they could cut me. Her arms were folded in front of her chest in a way that matched her confident air. Her long, voluminous hair was tied at the ends and fluttered defiantly in the breeze.

She was Philo, the chief’s granddaughter.

Great — I’d run into a nuisance.

“R-retract that! Brother isn’t an Otem maniac! Thanks to Father’s persuasion, recently he doesn’t make more than two Otems a day! He even pays attention to me! Right, brother? Right?” she blurted.

Well, turning that around means she thought I had been an Otem maniac before. So that’s how you saw me. I’m a little hurt, honestly.

“That is what being an Otem maniac means,” Philo said coldly.

“Relax. I didn’t come here to see you, anyway.”

“Wha—!? I— I never expected that and I don’t want that! That’s not what I meant—don’t say weird things!” Philo’s face flushed red as she shouted. She unfolded her arms and jabbed a finger at me. Kids are easy to tease.

“Besides, I know you’re the sort of pervert who only gets excited about Otems! I won’t fall for any of that!” she squealed.

I wanted to sock her. Is that really how you think of me? Who spread that ridiculous lie?

“I came to see my grandfather,” I said.

“Don’t call him ‘grandfather’ so casually, Otem maniac! Call him Chief!” she snapped.

“All right, I’ll call him ‘Chief’ in front of your grandfather,” I said.

The Maren spoke formally even to relatives; it made the air feel stifling. When you’re just two kids chatting, you’d like to be more relaxed.

“You, don’t you dare call my grandfather ‘grandfather’! Who do you think you are! I— I’m not in the least bit intending to become your wife! I don’t like you at all!” she went on.

“That’s not what I meant! I was just mocking the way you say it!” I replied, which only made things more awkward. Fine — from now on I’ll call him ‘Chief’ in front of Philo. Talking to her is exhausting.

“I’ve come to see the Chief,” I told her.

“Hmph. Whatever. I couldn’t care less what Abel wants. Just don’t embarrass me, okay? If you’re rude, I’ll throw you out myself — got it?” she said, then turned and walked ahead.

I waved her off with a light shoo as I faced the mansion and started toward the entrance. For some reason I could now hear three sets of footsteps. When I looked back, Philo was trailing right behind us.

“…Philo, this doesn’t concern you,” I said.

“I’m just going home! Don’t give me that look like I’m following you! This is my house, you know! I can go where I want!” she snapped.

She wasn’t a bad kid, but… well.

Still, I hadn’t met the Chief much. Maybe having Philo along would make things proceed some. If she wanted to stay, fine — no need to force her off.

We entered the mansion. At the genkan I ran into Philo’s mother. After exchanging brief greetings, I explained that I wanted to see the Chief.

“Of course. The Chief should be free today. Philo, why don’t you show him the way?” Philo’s mother said.

“Me? Well… I suppose I have to. I don’t want to, but fine, I’ll show him. I don’t want to, though,” Philo said dramatically.

“Sorry to trouble you, Philo. Please take him,” I said politely and bowed.

“Hah, that throws me off,” Philo muttered.

I keep this polite act whenever I’m around adults — it goes over better.

“I’m sorry about this, Abel. Philo’s not the easiest — but she’s a good girl at heart, so please be nice to her,” Philo’s mother urged, then left for another room while chuckling.

Philo cleared her throat and set off.

“Hey, what are you standing there for?” she snapped, and marched ahead of me down the corridor toward the Chief’s inner chambers.

I remembered the Chief as a rather crotchety old man. I’d planned only to make a quick appointment and leave, but since they led me straight in, that didn’t seem necessary.

After all, the Chief didn’t go hunting himself; the younger folk did. Nothing major had happened around the village lately, so he probably wasn’t that busy. And I was only seven. Maybe to them I was just like a visiting grandchild.

“Honestly, your mother is so careless. But I’m coming along, so you should be grateful — I’m taking time out of my life to escort you. I don’t know what you’ll ask of my grandfather, but don’t do anything to embarrass me. If you’re rude, I’ll kick you out, okay?” Philo rattled off, then looked straight ahead again.

You’d think she was just trying to walk home. I wanted to say that, but decided not to be petty.

The Chief was human after all. Having his granddaughter there might soften him a bit; it couldn’t hurt.
