Age Two

It’s been two years since I was given my second life.

I can walk around a little now, though to reach the second floor I still need to climb a ladder.
Since I’m rarely allowed outside, my whole world basically consists of the first floor of our house.

From what I’ve gathered over these past two years, I was reborn as the eldest son of the Berleck family, part of a race called the Maren.

My name is Abel — full name, Abel Berleck.

The white skin and red eyes are apparently common traits among the Maren people.
I had them too.
I had already guessed as much from looking at my parents, but even so, when I first saw myself in a mirror, I was speechless.

At first I was constantly panicking, but as time passed, I’ve come to accept my situation — at least somewhat.
That said, in my previous life I only had one parent, and he was a carefree idiot of a magician, so living with both parents now still feels strange to me.

I’ve started learning the language here, but I still have no clue where on Earth this country is supposed to be.
It’s a continent I’ve never even heard of.
Makes me wish I’d studied geography more seriously back in my old life.

I once asked Father for a world map, but he just laughed and said, “If such a thing existed, I’d want to see it myself.”

There’s nothing resembling electrical appliances in our home, either.
It seems like this place isn’t very developed.
I never thought there could still be somewhere like this in today’s world.
I miss my smartphone… and TV.

Every morning, my parents face a decorated window in their bedroom, close their eyes, and say,

“We pray to the spirits of our Maren ancestors,”
while tracing a cross in the air with their left hand.

They both do it with such solemn expressions that, the first time I saw it, I almost cried.

My new father often says, “Abel, you must also offer your prayers,” and tries to make me copy them.
For now, I take advantage of being a toddler and pretend not to understand.
But it’s only a matter of time before I’m expected to do it too.

Apparently, this prayer isn’t just for mornings — whenever you do something bad, you’re also supposed to trace a cross in the air with your left hand and say,

“Spirits of our Maren ancestors, forgive me.”

I don’t know the details, but supposedly the ancestral spirits will overlook most things after that.
Seems our Maren ancestors are a forgiving bunch.

Then there’s the food.

That’s been the biggest challenge.
Meals mostly consist of hard, black bread and something called “white soup,” which is like a thinned stew made from goat’s milk.

That much I can tolerate.
What I can’t handle are the occasional pig heads or the drinks made from boiled snake blood that appear on the table.
Apparently, they’re eaten during special ritual days.

The calendar system here is also strange — they use something called a day counter: a set of interlocking disks that you turn manually.
Every few rotations, the count shifts to adjust for something like leap years.
From the markings, I learned that there are twelve months in a year like Japan, but only 352 days.
That’s when it finally hit me — this might not even be Earth.

Aside from the food, there’s something else I can’t get used to: the Otems.

An Otem is a wooden, cylindrical statue — sort of like a carved drum with a face, sometimes with hands or wings.
They’re displayed everywhere, indoors and out.
Painted with strange makeup, they’re downright creepy.
What’s worse is that I have no idea what they’re for.
I’ve even dreamed of them chasing me.

At first, I thought they must be religious symbols, but then I saw Mother using one as a stepping stool to reach a book on a high shelf.
Still, afterward she traced a cross in the air and asked the ancestors for forgiveness, so maybe they are sacred after all.
But if she’s going to ask forgiveness every time, maybe she shouldn’t be doing it in the first place.
She’s the adult, not me.

“Is something wrong, Abel? You’ve been staring at the Otem for a while.”

Mother looked up from her chair, a book in her hand.
Even knowing she’s my biological mother, I still get nervous meeting her gaze.
How old is she, anyway? Probably not even thirty.

“Mother, what are the Otems for?”

I asked, gently touching one of them.

“Oh my, Abel, already interested in Otems? How promising!”

She smiled brightly.
So, these people really are that fond of Otems, huh?

“Otems are things your father, Lord Zele, makes for his magic training,” she explained.
“There are also some made for celebrations or decorations…”

Lord Zele — that’s Father.
His full name is Zeleit, but I’ve heard neighbors call him Zele or Mr. Zele, so it’s a nickname.
Still, I can’t help wondering why Mother uses an honorific when referring to him.
Maybe it’s a cultural thing — a way of showing respect to one’s husband.
But they seem affectionate enough that it feels more like a custom than formality.

Anyway, I wasn’t expecting magic to come up in the conversation.
So the Maren aren’t just religious — they actually believe in mystical arts too.
They really are behind the times.

I can’t help wondering if I’ll ever find a way back from this Maren village to Japan, where smartphones and TV exist.

“The one you’re touching now,” Mother continued, “was made to celebrate this child — little Giselle.”

She smiled softly and placed a hand on her rounded belly.

Yes — Mother was pregnant with her second child.

Apparently, through some kind of divination, they already knew it would be a girl.
Sounds dubious to me, but my parents wholeheartedly believed it.
I’m kind of curious to see their faces if it turns out wrong.

I moved closer to her, asked for permission, and gently placed my hand on her stomach.

If this baby ends up being another reincarnated soul like me…
maybe we’ll have some great conversations about our previous lives.
