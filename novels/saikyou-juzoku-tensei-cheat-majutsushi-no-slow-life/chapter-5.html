Age Six — Part 1

I was six; Giselle was four.

I started carving Otems two years ago—when I was four. Since then, I’ve made Otems almost every single day.

Storage became a problem, but I’m attached to the ones that turned out well. Father once tried to burn a whole batch at once, but I cried and protested until he relented and agreed to let part of the yard be an Otem storage area.

For once, I was glad to be a kid.

Father seems pleased that his son is so passionate about magical training, so he didn’t want to crush my enthusiasm. Giselle backing me up helped a lot, too. Father is weak to my little sister.

That said, our yard isn’t that big. Plenty of houses line up a few Otems out front, but none of them have a full-on trash-heap like ours. We definitely stand out, and Father doesn’t seem thrilled about that.

I do try to be selective—most pieces get smashed right after they’re finished—but the number keeps growing anyway. It used to take me over a week to make one. Now I can finish three a day. The yard is steadily being swallowed by Otems.

At this point, there are over a hundred lined up outside.

Today again, I sat on an Otem like a chair and carved another Otem. Giselle was beside me.

I told her she could sit on one too, but she shot back, “There's no way I could sit on something you made, brother!”

I actually made a few specifically for sitting, so I’d have appreciated it if she used them…

Still, I’m carving every day—and she’s… watching me carve, every day. Her pale cheeks faintly pink, she looks on happily while I work. Doesn’t she get bored just watching?

I keep thinking I should teach her to carve, but… she’s my cute little sister, and putting a blade in her hands makes me hesitate. Maybe when she turns five— And only after discussing it with Father. If I give her a knife behind his back, he might deck me when he finds out.

“…Abel. Abel, my son.”

Right as I was thinking about Father, he called to me with a worried tone. I’d been so lost in thought I hadn’t noticed him approach. Startled, I almost dropped the knife, but snatched it out of the air at the last second.

“Y-Yes, Father?”

“Why do you keep making Otems so obsessively?”

“Well… because it’s the best training for magic…”

In this Maren settlement, being a skilled magic-user is a status symbol. You’d think parents would be happy their child trains day in, day out—but lately, when my parents look at me, there’s a clear hint of confusion.

“Even so… well. I didn’t make my first Otem until I was eight. I won’t say it’s too early for you—but you could stand to be a little more childlike and just… play.”

So that’s it. He’s worried I’m not acting like a kid.

From my perspective, spending all your time on magical training is way more childish—but saying that in a world with real magic won’t get me anywhere.

Besides, if you add my past life to this one, I’m comfortably over twenty. I can’t bring myself to run around playing with the village children. I’d much rather carve Otems and immerse myself in magic.

If we had games or smartphones, maybe I’d think differently—but I doubt this world has anything like that.

“For example, how about going to play in the forest outside the settlement? You can’t take Giselle yet—it’s too dangerous—so I can’t approve that…”

The forest outside the village, huh.

And what exactly am I supposed to do for fun there? Maybe running around is enough for a child, but mentally, that phase is long behind me.

For a moment I consider telling him about my previous life—but I stop myself. If they found me creepy and drove me out of the village—or worse, called me demon-possessed and executed me—it’d be the end. Even if it didn’t go that far, I don’t want things to get awkward with my family.

I love my parents and my sister now. They’re just as precious to me as my family from my previous life.

“Well, even so—”
“N-No! I don’t want that! If brother goes, then I—I’m going too!”

Giselle grabbed the hem of my clothes and clutched tight. She’s surprisingly strong. Hey, you’ll wrinkle it.

“M-Mm…”

Now it was Father’s turn to be at a loss. He really is weak to her.

“Father, Father! You always say to get along with brother—so why would you say something like that now?!”

And there she goes—tears.

Giselle is a hopeless brother’s girl who sticks to me all day, and she’s only four. Saying you’re going to separate us is a one-way ticket to a tantrum. That one’s on Father.

I stroked her back to calm her down, then shot Father a deliberately sharp glare.

“Th-There, there—stop crying! It’s my fault, all right? Father was wrong!”

Good. Crisis averted.

As I soothed her, I also patted her back in thanks. Sobbing hiccups—hic, hic—she leaned into me and wrapped her arms tightly around my waist.

While Father floundered, I stuck my tongue out at him where he couldn’t see.

I’ll use Giselle as my shield for a while. I don’t like the idea of hurting her feelings, so next time I’ll prep things in advance and catch Father in a contradiction. Giselle’s always by my side; she’ll make an excellent shield anytime… not that it sounds very nice when I put it that way.
