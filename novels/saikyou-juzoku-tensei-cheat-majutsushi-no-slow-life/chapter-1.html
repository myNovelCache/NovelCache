I raise my head and rub my eyes.

Drool had stuck to my cheek — looks like I’d fallen asleep face-down on the desk again.

So I’d blacked out in the middle of reading another book.

It’s not a healthy habit, but there’s no one around to scold me for it.
My mother was already gone by the time I was old enough to remember anything, and my father was stabbed to death when I was little.
The grandfather who took me in afterward passed away from old age last year, so now I live alone.

My father had been a somewhat well-known magician in his line of work — a quirky man who loved to travel.
He’d wander overseas without warning, leaving his kid behind, and try to make it up to me with strange souvenirs that made no sense.

His catchphrase on stage was, “I am a real magician!”
Apparently, part of his act involved handing a knife to a planted audience member and letting them pretend to stab him.
But one day, an overly excited spectator pulled out their own knife and slashed him for real.
He never regained consciousness — gone just like that.

As a kid, I really believed he was a real magician, so I thought he’d come back one day from somewhere far away.
When I asked Grandpa, “When is Dad coming home?” he would just narrow his eyes silently and pat my head.
Now that I think about it, he must’ve been pitying me.

My gaze drifts to the book I’d been reading.

“The Complete Compendium of Medieval Black Magic.”

As much as I hate to admit it, my interest in these shady topics definitely comes from all those “souvenirs” Dad used to bring me.

This suspicious tome isn’t the only thing — my house is full of sketchy black-magic kits.
I know there’s no such thing as magic, but part of me still wishes there were.
Seventeen years old now, and that childish dream has only earned me the title of chūnibyō — a cringey wannabe “dark mage.”

No one at school talks to me. Most just stare like I’m some rare bug.

The only people I can really call acquaintances are the members of the Black Magic Research Club — though since our senior Yama-san retired to focus on exams, it’s just me and my junior, Ii-chan, left.
Which means, sadly enough, Ii-chan is my only conversation partner.

And according to school policy, if no new members join next year, our little “evil” club will be disbanded. Evil, it seems, always perishes.

This club is my only place of belonging.
I have to protect it at all costs.

It’s about time I held a meeting with Ii-chan to plan how we’ll recruit new members.
If even one of the black-magic kits I bought online turned out to be the real deal, I bet people would come running to join us.
Not that I actually believe that’ll ever happen.

The incident happened after school.

“…I think I’m gonna quit this club.”

After classes ended, my trusted junior slammed me with that statement.

Looking back, it was a miracle someone as kind, pretty, and sensible as Ii-chan had stuck around this weird club for so long.

But knowing that didn’t make it any easier to accept.

I wanted at least to convince her to take a break instead of quitting — or, if nothing else, to keep her name on the roster for appearances’ sake.
But she wouldn’t hear any of it.

By the time I’d recovered from the shock and tried to talk things through, she was already walking out of the clubroom.

I managed to catch up near the school gate, but even then, the conversation went nowhere.

In the heavy silence, Ii-chan suddenly turned away and started walking faster — right into the crosswalk.

The light was red.
She probably hadn’t noticed it change, distracted by me.

A large truck was coming through the intersection.

Before I knew it, I was running.

A crushing impact hit my body, and everything went dark.

Hot. My whole body felt like it was burning.

I forced my eyes open, but my vision was fuzzy — no, it felt like my brain itself was failing.

Voices rose all around me. The noise was annoying, somehow distant.

Someone was clinging to me.

“N-No! No! I’m sorry, I’m so sorry! It’s all my fault—!”

Even through the haze, I recognized my junior’s voice.

Good. She’s alive.

It wouldn’t have looked cool if we’d both died.

Better the outcast dies than the popular one — fewer people to mourn me anyway.

Lucky, in a twisted way. I’ve got no family left. Hardly any friends.

My only regrets are that I never finished researching alchemy history… and that the “dragon’s claw” I ordered from overseas was supposed to arrive today. Pathetic, really.

I didn’t even have a dream for the future.

Those were my last thoughts before everything went black.
