Age Six — Part 2

Mother taught me how to read and write, so I can already handle basic literacy.

Well, “taught” might be too generous a word. She just wrote out a list of characters on paper, read them aloud to me a few times, and left me with that. After that, I learned almost everything by myself by comparing that chart with books.

Mother was enthusiastic about teaching, but perhaps because she saw me as a six-year-old, her pace was painfully slow. In the Maren tribe, it seems learning to read and write by the age of ten is considered sufficient. She probably thought I was rushing it.

Even when I said, “I already know this,” she would repeat the same lesson, or go backward after barely making progress. So after the first day, I pretended to get bored and said, “Maybe that’s enough for now,” and secretly continued studying on my own.

Now, I was sitting on an Otem in the yard, reading a beginner’s book about magic. It was filled with illustrations — clearly made for children. That way, if someone saw me, I could claim I was just looking at the pictures, not reading.

My parents still didn’t know I could actually read fluently. If Mother found out that I’d mastered it alone after quitting her lessons, she’d probably feel a bit conflicted as a mother. And since Father had just told me I wasn’t acting like a child, it was better to keep it secret. Later, I could just say I’d learned simple letters from looking at picture books.

“Brother, can you read letters?”

Giselle craned her neck to peek at the book beside me.

“Yeah — but don’t tell Father or Mother, all right?”

“Amazing! You’re amazing! Oh, can you read me The Tale of the Little People’s Forest?”

That was a collection of fairy tales — the one Mother often read aloud to us before bed.

“I don’t want to get caught reading, remember? Why don’t you have Mother read it to you?”

“B-but… I want you to read it to me, brother. Is that… not allowed?”

Persistent, huh.

“All right, then. But let’s make it our secret — just between you and me.”

“Just between… us?”

“That’s right. You don’t mind, do you?”

“I—I don’t mind! I understand! It’ll be our special secret, brother!”

Good. Perfectly handled. Giselle was happy, and everyone else remained none the wiser.

The main reason I wanted to learn to read was so I could begin serious magical study. Otem carving is the foundation, but after years of it, I was ready to move on. Father still said I was too young to learn proper spells, but I wanted to get ahead by studying on my own.

According to the book, the most basic forms of magic were clairvoyance and fortune-telling using divination cards.

These divination cards were like a Maren-exclusive version of tarot. There were red and black suits, thirteen cards each, numbered from 1 to 13, plus a colorless “zero” card — twenty-seven in total. Each card bore an illustration — a thief, a hunter, a magician, and other mysterious figures. Aside from color, cards of the same number shared the same image.

Their use resembled tarot, but physically they were more like playing cards. In fact, Giselle and I once used them to play Old Maid.

…We got scolded harshly by Father afterward. Apparently, they weren’t meant for games.

If he wants me to act like a child, he could at least overlook something like that. Instead, we had to do that usual gesture — tracing a cross in the air and praying for forgiveness.

If I tried bringing out the cards again, Father would definitely say something. Maybe I should have Giselle fetch them for me.

When we played Old Maid, Father had yelled, “You were the one who put her up to it, weren’t you?!” and scolded only me. He was right, of course, so I didn’t complain — but the man just couldn’t bring himself to punish Giselle. That’s why it’s better if she’s the one to get them.

“Giselle, do you remember the divination cards?”

“Yes, I do. The ones we used for Old Maid…”

So in her mind, they were permanently branded as Old Maid cards. Well, fine. It’s not like I’m in a position to correct her. If only “divination card Old Maid” would catch on enough that Father stopped getting mad about it.

“Right. I need you to go upstairs and bring me those cards. I’ll distract Mother downstairs.”

“Yes! Leave it to me, brother!”

She agreed without hesitation. Good girl — a truly dependable little sister.

Still, if I’m going to use the divination cards, I’d better do it somewhere Father can’t see. Once I have Mother’s permission, I’ll head to a quiet spot away from the settlement.
