Age Seven — Part 1

A full year had passed since I began training clairvoyance with the divination cards.

I was now seven.
Giselle was five.

When I focused on the back of a card and channeled my intent, the image on the front would now appear faintly in my mind’s eye.
I could guess every card correctly — one hundred percent accuracy.

Apparently, even among adult Maren, few could reach that level.
I was glad I had trained from such a young age — perhaps I had a natural talent for it as well.

Still, this ability worked only because the divination cards themselves held magical energy.
It wasn’t like I could see through walls or anything.
It was useful only as a training method, not for any real-world purpose.

So no, I couldn’t use it to peek at people bathing or changing clothes.
There were spells and magic circles that supposedly allowed such sight, but I wasn’t capable of casting those yet.

“প্রেত光よ　আঁকা描け”

I recited from the magic book, raising my hand toward the air.

This was spirit language — an incantation that called upon the spirits in the atmosphere to assist in manifesting magic.

Complex spells, it seemed, required three components:
your own magic power as fuel, the spell (the words) to call spirits, and a magic circle to issue detailed commands to them.

In short, pure magic power could only produce simple effects.
By adding an incantation, one could influence spirits and cause physical phenomena like fire,
and by adding a magic circle, that phenomenon could be freely shaped or controlled.

There were exceptions, of course, but generally:

Magic power = fuel or wage

Incantation = verbal command

Magic circle = blueprint

The spell I was casting was one that drew shapes in the air — a practice spell for inscribing magic circles.
It allowed the spirits to trace the image in my mind into the air using gathered light.
Since it only involved manipulating light, it didn’t require a magic circle itself.

Drawing with ink worked just as well, but once you got used to this, it was far faster —
though accuracy and speed depended heavily on the caster’s skill.

Today’s exercise, however, wasn’t to draw a magic circle.
I was drawing Giselle’s face.

After about ten minutes, the shimmering outline was complete — from the sheen of her cheeks to the soft texture of her hair, all recreated in glowing air.

Success.

Last time, her face had come out stretched sideways and fat, and she’d burst into tears.
Apparently, she didn’t mind being my Otem model, but she did mind being drawn plump.
Maybe it was worse because it still looked somewhat like her.

Ah, the burdens of an older brother — having to balance training and entertaining your little sister.
Not that I really minded. It was fun.

“It looks just like me!”

Giselle clapped happily, laughing and spinning around.
I smiled in satisfaction, then cut off the magic.

The glowing portrait dissolved into the air.

“Ah…”

She let out a small, sad sigh.

Sorry, Giselle — maintaining the image eats up a lot of magic power.

At least it was good practice.

Simple figures could be drawn in ten seconds or less,
but even then, I couldn’t imagine this being practical for combat or daily use.

Just firing one fireball took an absurd amount of preparation.

First, a spell to draw the magic circle.
Then, another to ignite the flame.

“প্রেত光よ　আঁকা描け”

I spoke again, raising my right hand and focusing on the diagram in the book before me —
a magic circle for shaping and launching a contained fireball.

It didn’t create fire itself, only formed a conduit for it.
To ignite the flame, another incantation was required.

Spirits were terribly literal creatures.
They seemed to understand the incantations but lacked will or intellect — more like instinctive responses than true thought.

According to my books, spirits were formed from the lingering thoughts of living beings,
mixed fragments of souls bound to the land or objects.
Everywhere in the world contained them, in varying densities.

“শিখা炎よ　এই হাত球を象れ”

I needed to keep the power low — I didn’t want to set the forest on fire.

Flame gathered in my palm, forming a small sphere.
I launched it straight ahead.
It hit the ground, flared, then vanished with a brief crackle.

“W-wow! Amazing!”

This was the first time Giselle had seen me use actual fire magic.

Up until now, I’d only practiced when I could be alone.
It was too risky — I didn’t want to hurt her.

She squealed with joy, but I wasn’t satisfied.

“Hmmm…”

It took me nearly twenty seconds to perform the entire sequence.

In my fantasy logic, that’s long enough for a goblin to beat me to death before the spell fires.
Kind of kills the dream when you think about it realistically.

Honestly, a bow and arrow would be far more efficient.

Even if I trained to cast faster, for something like hunting, arrows would do just fine.

There had been wars in the distant past, but judging by this peaceful village, that age was long gone.
No one was firing spells at each other anymore.

And if everyone could use magic anyway, it wasn’t even impressive — it was just… ordinary.

Wait—
…Is that why nobody here takes magic that seriously?

N-no, that can’t be it.
It’s magic, for crying out loud.

I’ve devoted my entire childhood to this!

…Better not overthink it.

No — this will be useful somehow.
I’ve already come too far to stop.
I have to master magic.

Still, the books at home could only take me so far.
The basics were fine, but I wanted to try more advanced techniques — things the texts didn’t fully explain.
I had too many questions only experience could answer.

It was about time I found myself a teacher.

Maybe I should ask Father.
