Age Four

Another two years passed, and I turned four.

By now, I was fully accustomed to life as a member of the Maren tribe.
I’d even grown to enjoy the white soup I once thought was just glorified water, and I could gulp down heated snake blood without flinching.
A pig’s head on the dinner table no longer fazed me in the slightest.

I was long since used to my pale-skinned relatives and my own light-colored appearance, and my hobby had become crafting Otems.
Every morning, I stood before the sacred window to offer prayers.
At this point, going back to my old life might actually be harder than staying here.

The longer I lived among the Maren, the more convinced I became that this world wasn’t Earth.

I lowered my gaze from the sky to the log in my hands.
Today again, I sat in the yard beside our house, carving the surface of a log with a knife to make an Otem.

The carving knife bore a Maren ritual inscription — it resonated with the magical energy in the surrounding trees, making it easy even for a child to carve.

“Brother, brother! Please play with me, brother!”

My little sister Giselle tugged at my sleeve.

She was two years younger than me — meaning she was two now.
She looked like Mother, with large eyes and long lashes that made them stand out even more.
Her hair and skin were white, her eyes red — like every Maren.

Giselle’s ethereal beauty mixed with childish innocence made her look like a porcelain doll.
When she slept, I sometimes wondered if she was one.

For some reason, my parents always left her in my care.
Sure, I’m her older brother — but I’m still only four.
Sometimes I think they’re pushing a bit too much responsibility on me. Maybe that’s just my old Japanese mindset clashing with Maren culture.

It’s not like our parents are too busy, nor do they seem neglectful.
Once, I even caught them smiling warmly from a distance as Giselle and I played together.
Maybe they just wanted us to grow close as siblings.

“Brother? Are you listening, brother?”

“I’m focusing on making an Otem right now. I’ll play with you when I’m done.”

“…Why are you so obsessed with those Otems, brother?”

“It’s not the Otems themselves — it’s what comes after them.”

“After… them?”

“Yeah. Otems are the foundation of all magical training. I’m interested in magic.”

Yes — this tribe could actually use magic, the very thing I had longed for in my previous life.
From what Father told me, it seemed that magic was a universally recognized phenomenon in this world.
That was the biggest reason I was sure this wasn’t Earth.

When I first realized magic was real, I got so excited I ran laps around the room.
Reincarnation and real magic?
It was just like a light novel or web novel come to life!

For the record, shouting “Status Open!” did absolutely nothing.
That was disappointing.
Mother just gave me a confused look, and I nearly cried.

When Father first started talking about rituals and magic, I thought it was all superstition — but once I saw it with my own eyes, I couldn’t deny it.
He could heal small wounds, sense emotions — things that, in my old world, would’ve been dismissed as cheap tricks.

I questioned him endlessly, certain there had to be a gimmick or illusion behind it, but found none.
It didn’t take long for me to conclude: magic really existed.

Father seemed genuinely surprised by my skepticism.
To him, magic was just… normal.
And I guess it made sense — if you grew up in a world where it existed, it was as natural as breathing.

In my past life, I’d spent years yearning for real magic — and now that it was in front of me, I doubted it at first.
I suppose that makes me a fairly rational person after all.

When Father proudly said, “Look, Abel! This is magic!” and healed a wound before my eyes, I was stunned into silence.
I was amazed — both by the existence of magic and by how casually he treated it, like a party trick.
Giselle, meanwhile, just clapped and giggled, no different from seeing a new toy.

“I still don’t understand why you’re so obsessed with magic, brother,”

Giselle puffed her cheeks.
Clearly, she was sulking from being ignored.

But from my point of view, how could she not be obsessed?
How could anyone not care?

Magic. Real magic!
Mystical, impossible power — right here, in our world!
If that doesn’t ignite passion, what could possibly do it?

Giselle took it for granted. Magic was too normal for her to appreciate.

Even our parents were the same.
If only they’d look after Giselle for a bit while I focused on my studies!
You’d think Father, of all people, would be thrilled I was so dedicated to the craft.

Honestly, this tribe lacked ambition.
With real magic in their grasp, they should be training every spare second!

“Brother, brother! Yesterday, when I went outside with Mother, I saw Lady Philo with a Miawolf! I want a Miawolf too!”

Giselle changed the topic — clearly trying to catch my attention.

Philo was the great-granddaughter (or maybe just granddaughter — the language didn’t distinguish) of the Maren chieftain.
Vain, arrogant, and always picking fights — not exactly my favorite person.

The Miawolf was a small wolf species — white fur, red eyes, and a temperament that mirrored the Maren: calm and obedient.
The tribe often tamed them for hunting companions.

“Then you should ask Father.”

“Mmm…”

So that was her plan — to get me to indulge her.
Sorry, but I needed to focus.

Otem carving was the foundation of magical training for a reason.
It developed concentration, spatial awareness, color sense, and imagination — all essential for magic use.

While some Otems were carved from stone or minerals, wood was preferred for training.
Wood conducted magical energy well and was tied to nature’s life force, helping to boost one’s own magic.
At least, that’s what one of our thick old books said — in a roundabout, pompous way.

According to Father, the trees around our settlement were naturally infused with magic, further enhancing our training.
With such perfect conditions for learning, there was no excuse for slacking off.

Now, how could I get Giselle to stay quiet?
Ah — I had an idea.

“Hey, Giselle. How about I make you the model for my next Otem?”

“R-Really, brother!?”

Her eyes sparkled, and she flapped her little hands excitedly.
Too easy. Should’ve started with that.

“Sit on that stone for me. Yes, perfect. Don’t move too much — I want to see your face clearly.”

“Okay! Okay!”

In an instant, the restless little girl sat up perfectly straight on the stone, as stiff as a statue — practically holding her breath.
By the time I finished, she’d probably be exhausted.

Not that it mattered — Otems only vaguely resembled humans anyway.
Still, I’d have to make it look enough like her to keep her happy.

I kept glancing up at her as I carved, looking for any defining features to copy.
Each time I did, her pale cheeks turned pink and she shyly looked away.
Maybe I was staring too much.

The result, as usual, was a typical Otem — thick, lumpy, and ugly, with no resemblance to Giselle whatsoever.
But since she looked delighted holding it, I decided that was good enough.

As I watched her joyfully hugging the misshapen statue and thanking me over and over, I couldn’t shake a faint sense of dissatisfaction.
Still… if she was happy, that was what mattered.
